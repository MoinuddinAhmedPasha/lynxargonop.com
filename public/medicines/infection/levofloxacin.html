Levofloxacin

A potent fluoroquinolone antibiotic used for serious bacterial infections.

What is Levofloxacin?

Levofloxacin is a third-generation fluoroquinolone antibiotic and the active L-isomer of ofloxacin.

It offers stronger and more targeted antibacterial activity.

History & Background

Levofloxacin was developed to improve the effectiveness and safety profile of earlier fluoroquinolones by isolating the active enantiomer.

Chemical Structure & Properties

  • IUPAC name: (S)-9-fluoro-2,3-dihydro-3-methyl-10-(4-methylpiperazin-1-yl)-7-oxo-7H-pyrido[1,2,3-de][1,4]benzoxazine-6-carboxylic acid
  • Molecular formula: C₁₈H₂₀FN₃O₄
  • Molar mass: 361.37 g/mol
  • Functional groups: Fluoroquinolone core, carboxylic acid

Mechanism of Action

Levofloxacin kills bacteria by:

  • Inhibiting DNA gyrase
  • Blocking topoisomerase IV
  • Preventing bacterial DNA replication

Pharmacokinetics

  • Absorption: Excellent oral absorption (~99%)
  • Peak plasma time: 1–2 hours
  • Half-life: 6–8 hours
  • Metabolism: Minimal
  • Excretion: Urine (unchanged)

Medical Uses

  • Community-acquired pneumonia
  • Urinary tract infections
  • Skin and soft tissue infections
  • Sinusitis
  • Prostatitis

Dosage Guidelines

Adults: 250–750 mg once daily

Duration: Depends on infection type

⚠️ Avoid unnecessary use to prevent resistance.

Side Effects

  • Nausea
  • Dizziness
  • Headache
  • Tendon pain or rupture

Warnings & Precautions

  • Risk of tendon rupture (elderly)
  • Avoid in pregnancy unless essential
  • QT prolongation risk

Drug Interactions

  • Antacids and iron supplements
  • Antiarrhythmic drugs
  • Corticosteroids (↑ tendon risk)

Frequently Asked Questions

Is levofloxacin stronger than ofloxacin? – Yes.

Can it treat viral infections? – No.

Is it safe long-term? – Short-term use preferred.

Why is it restricted? – Safety concerns.
